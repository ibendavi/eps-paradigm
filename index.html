The Big Picture

One Objective, Three Classic Problems

Every CEO must answer three questions: How should I finance existing assets? Which new projects should I start? When should I return profits to shareholders? These are the three classic problems in corporate finance—and real-world CEOs solve all three with an eye toward EPS.

⚖

Capital Structure

Fund assets with the cheapest capital. Equity costs the earnings yield; debt costs the interest rate. Tilt toward whichever demands less earnings.

⚙

Real Investment

A project is accretive if its income yield exceeds the firm's cheapest financing cost. The hurdle rate = min{EY, i, rf}.

❖

Payout Policy

Return cash when you can't invest it accretively. Repurchases generate an accretive pop; dividends bypass the income statement entirely.

★

Value vs. Growth

Value stocks: EY > rf → equity is expensive, lever up. Growth stocks: EY < rf → equity is cheap, stay unlevered.

☼

Asset Pricing (Investor Side)

Analysts don't discount future cash flows. They multiply E[EPS] × Trailing P/E. Pricing is backward-looking, not forward-looking.

1

EPS-Maximizing Capital Structure

There is an optimal leverage even without frictions. Why? Because an EPS maximizer and her creditors measure their slices of the pie differently.

Modigliani & Miller (1958) proved capital structure is irrelevant when everyone measures value the same way. But an EPS maximizer cares only about next year's interest payment, while bond markets price the present value of all future payments. Different yardsticks → different optimal slicing.

Key trade-off: If EY > i · (1 + δ) → equity is expensive, lever up. If EY < i · (1 + δ) → debt is expensive, reduce leverage.

The bright line: Whether a firm's unlevered earnings yield is above or below the risk-free rate determines its entire strategy.

Growth Stocks (EY < rf)

  • Zero financial leverage — even riskfree debt is too expensive
  • Rarely repurchase shares — no margin to readjust
  • Rapidly accumulate cash — lending at rf is a great deal
  • Issue equity to fund operations

Value Stocks (EY > rf)

  • High leverage — borrow up to riskfree capacity and beyond
  • Actively repurchase shares when EY rises above i
  • Spend cash quickly — lending at rf is a bad deal
  • Issue debt regularly to fund operations

Empirical evidence: Value stocks carry 10.2 percentage points more leverage, are 19.8 pp more likely to have nonzero financial leverage, repurchase shares and issue debt at roughly double the rate, and accumulate cash 3× slower than growth stocks.

2

Capital Budgeting for EPS Maximizers

Maximizing EPS ≠ minimizing investment. EPS maximizers use real investment to arbitrage between asset and capital markets.

The conventional wisdom is that EPS fixation leads to chronic underinvestment. IBM's CEO laid out an explicit EPS roadmap in 2006—and invested his way to 17%/year EPS growth. "Growth Initiatives & Acquisitions" accounted for $2.10/share of IBM's $5.47/share gain. Only $1.30 came from share repurchases.

The EPS investment rule: Invest if Income Yield > Financing Yield = min{EY, i, rf}

IRRs and payback periods fit naturally: An IRR is a multi-period income yield. A payback period is just 1/IY quoted as a multiple. EPS maximizers like short payback periods for the same reason they like acquiring targets at a low P/E multiple.

  • M&A payment method: Cash deals when rf or i are cheap; stock deals when EY is cheap
  • Investment-cash flow sensitivity: Strongest for value stocks whose cheapest funding is internal cash
  • Convertible bonds: Growth stocks issue convertibles to access cheap quasi-equity financing
  • Capitalized interest: During construction, EPS maximizers capitalize rather than expense interest
3

EPS-Maximizing Payout Policy

Cash retention has a cost: the earnings yield. Return cash when you can't beat that yield by investing. Repurchases are accretive; dividends are not.

Retain vs. Return: Cash Yield (CY) = (1 − θ) · rf + θ · IY
If CY > EY → keep cash. If CY < EY → return cash to shareholders.

The fundamental tension: A high excess earnings yield (EY − rf) makes internal cash cheap to spend, but the same spread makes buybacks more attractive. The companies that benefit most from cash-funded projects face the strongest pressure to distribute.

Growth Stocks (EY < rf)

  • Always retain cash — lending at rf clears their low EY hurdle
  • Never return cash to shareholders
  • Fund projects with equity, not cash
  • Cash builds up on the balance sheet

Value Stocks (EY > rf)

  • Return cash unless enough high-yield projects exist
  • Prefer repurchases (accretive pop)
  • Cash is their cheapest capital, but retention hurdle is high
  • Marginal value stocks (EY ≈ rf) are most likely to pay dividends

Why dividends are dominated: Repurchases and dividends both deliver the same value to shareholders. But only repurchases boost EPS. Hence, EPS maximizers always prefer buybacks. When a firm pays dividends anyway, it must be due to forces outside the model—and the prediction is this happens most for marginal value stocks where the accretive pull of buybacks is weakest.

4

Expected EPS × Trailing P/E: Pricing Without Discounting

The investor side of the paradigm. How do investors actually price stocks? Not by discounting future cash flows. They multiply expected earnings times a backward-looking multiple.

FINRA Rule 2241 requires analysts to explain their valuation method in every report. In a sample of 513 analyst reports, 76.8% set price targets by multiplying expected EPS times the company's trailing P/E ratio. Only 30.2% even mention DCF analysis.

How analysts actually price: Price Target = E[EPS] × Trailing P/E

Key insight: A trailing P/E is backward-looking. The Gordon model's "correct" multiple is forward-looking: 1/(r − g). But analysts don't use it. They ask: "How did the market price last year's earnings?" This is yield-based pricing using comps, not discounted cash flow.

  • 94.5% of reports use some form of trailing multiple
  • Trailing PEs produce price targets that are roughly correct on average
  • The simple model predicts how price targets and market prices respond to earnings news
  • EPS surprises mechanically move prices because analysts re-apply the same multiple to updated earnings
Tying It Together

The Max EPS Paradigm: A Complete Framework

The five papers in this research program form a unified alternative to the textbook NPV paradigm. Two sides of the same coin:

Manager Side: Max EPS

CEOs take the earnings yield as the market price of equity. If EY exceeds the borrowing rate, equity is "expensive"—so they lever up and repurchase shares. All corporate policies (leverage, payout, M&A, investment) are means to boost EPS.

Investor Side: EPS × P/E

Investors capitalize current earnings using comparables. Nobody computes NPV—they use cap rates, comps, and multiples. The discount rate is the earnings yield itself, observable from price and earnings.

Pre-1958: The Capitalization Era

Graham, Solomon, Dean, Berle all assumed earnings capitalization. EPS was the default framework for corporate finance.

1958: Modigliani & Miller

Changed the academic objective function from EPS to NPV. The irrelevance theorems assume everyone uses present-value logic.

1958–Present: The Friction Industry

Since NPV explains nothing without frictions, academics spent decades adding taxes, information asymmetry, agency costs, etc.

2024–Present: The Max EPS Paradigm

Ben-David & Chinco propose restoring the original objective. Not because MM's logic is wrong, but because managers and investors use a different system.
